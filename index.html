Overview

HOR is a forum to present work concerning all aspects of higher-order rewriting. The aim is to provide an informal and friendly setting to discuss recent work and work in progress. The following is a non-exhaustive list of topics for the workshop:

  • Applications: proof checking, theorem proving, generic programming, declarative programming, program transformation, automated termination/confluence/equivalence analysis tools.
  • Foundations: pattern matching, unification, strategies, narrowing, termination, syntactic properties, type theory, complexity of derivations.
  • Frameworks: term rewriting, conditional rewriting, graph rewriting, net rewriting, comparisons of different frameworks.
  • Implementation: explicit substitution, rewriting tools, compilation techniques.
  • Semantics: semantics of higher-order rewriting, categorical rewriting, higher-order abstract syntax, games and rewriting

Submission

To give a presentation at the workshop, please submit an extended abstract (between 2 to 5 pages) via Easychair:

https://easychair.org/conferences/?conf=hor2023

Please use LaTeX and the Easychair style to prepare your submission:

https://easychair.org/publications/easychair.zip

HOR is a platform for discussing open questions, ongoing research, and new perspectives, as well as new results. Extended abstracts describing work in progress, preliminary results, research projects, or problems in higher-order rewriting are very welcome. Specifically, short versions of recently published papers are welcome, and submission to HOR does not preclude formal publication at other venues.

The workshop has informal electronic proceedings that will be made available on this website.

For questions regarding submission, please contact the PC chair Carsten Fuhs.

Invited speaker

Pablo Barenbaum – Universidad de Buenos Aires, Argentina

Title:
Useful Evaluation, Compositionally and Quantitatively

Abstract:
The question of measuring the complexity of functional programming languages (mathematically modeled by the lambda-calculus) in a machine-independent way has a long history. In particular, well-known implementation techniques turn to be unreasonable, in the sense of van Emde Boas' Invariance Thesis. Indeed, the evaluation of expressions in functional programming languages may lead to an exponential explosion in size. A significant contribution to this topic is due to Accattoli and Dal Lago, who proposed a reasonable cost model for functional programming languages, called useful evaluation. This implementation technique uses directed acyclic graphs providing succint representations of expressions by means of sharing subterms. In such a way, exponential size explosion can be avoided, by copying shared subterms only when this contributes to a certain (well-defined notion of) progress of the computation. The existing notions of useful evaluation in the literature were defined in an ad-hoc (non inductive and not compositional) way. Indeed, they are not characterized by local predicates, but rather by means of side conditions of a global nature. This makes it hard to reason about them using inductive arguments. Moreover, no quantitative models or tools exist to measure the time complexity of useful evaluation.

In this joint work with Delia Kesner and Mariana Milicich we provide the first inductive specification of useful evaluation for open call-by-value. This is achieved by indexing the notion of evaluation by different parameters representing the relevant information concerning the surrounding evaluation contexts. We relate our new specification of usefulness to a natural specification of open call-by-value that does not implement useful evaluation, showing in particular that both compute the same normal forms. Most importantly, we provide a model for the useful call-by-value evaluation strategy, which is formulated by means of a type system, based on intersection types. This turns out to be the first model for a notion of useful evaluation. Moreover, by taking intersection types to be non-idempotent, and the typing rules to be tight, the resulting model is quantitative, in the sense that typing derivations provide information about the exact number of computation steps required to evaluate the terms.
